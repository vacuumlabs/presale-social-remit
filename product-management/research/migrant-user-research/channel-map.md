# Channel and GTM map

## Main GTM insight

For UK-Ghana and UK-Nigeria remittance, acquisition is not only a digital marketing
problem. Remittance is embedded in community trust, family obligation, receiver
constraints, and corridor-specific proof. A new app can be digital-first, but early trust
probably needs social proof from people and places the community already trusts.

## Channel categories

### Diaspora community and faith networks

Churches, mosques, hometown associations, alumni groups, professional associations, and
ethnic/community groups can act as trust bridges. They matter because the core user risk
is not "can I download the app?" but "will my money arrive and will someone help if it
doesn't?"

For Ghana specifically, historical UK-Ghana remittance work points to diaspora and
hometown associations as part of the informal/social landscape around sending. For Nigeria,
UCL research on remittance entrepreneurship highlights ethnic networks and personal trust
as part of informal operator resilience.

GTM implication: SocialRemit should test small, corridor-specific community launches
instead of relying only on app-store acquisition. The promise should be demonstrated
through live transfers, receiver stories, and transparent support.

### Money-transfer shops and diaspora businesses

Even if SocialRemit is app-first, shops remain important as trust anchors and comparison
points. They are where some users have learned how remittance works, where cash-in
expectations come from, and where informal advice travels.

GTM implication: map diaspora businesses in London, Birmingham, Manchester, and other
high-density communities. The question is not necessarily whether SocialRemit needs
agents in MVP; the question is whether physical/community touchpoints are needed for
trust, support, referral, or assisted onboarding.

### WhatsApp and Facebook groups

WhatsApp groups are particularly important for Nigerian informal-transfer discourse:
brokered swaps and trusted contacts appear in public discussions as cost-effective
alternatives. Facebook groups and community pages likely matter for Ghanaian and Nigerian
diaspora announcements, but quality varies and access may require manual community entry.

GTM implication: WhatsApp/Facebook are less like ad channels and more like trust
networks. Spammy acquisition will likely fail. Referral mechanics, trusted ambassadors,
and community administrators matter.

### TikTok, Instagram, and creator/influencer channels

Short-form video can reach younger senders and newly arrived migrants, especially for
rate comparison, "how to send money home", and diaspora finance content. The risk is that
fintech messaging can slide into generic claims. Corridor-specific proof will matter:
"UK to Ghana MoMo arrived in X minutes" is stronger than "fast global transfers."

GTM implication: creator content should show realistic use cases: emergency transfer,
school-fee support, MoMo payout, Nigerian bank payout, failed-transfer recovery, and rate
comparison. Avoid over-polished brand claims.

### Referral and family-led acquisition

The receiver can influence the sender's choice by saying which payout method works, which
provider is trusted locally, and whether cash, bank, or mobile money is most convenient.
This creates a two-sided referral opportunity: sender invites receiver, receiver confirms
preferred payout, sender repeats.

GTM implication: recipient-side confirmation and referral loops may be more powerful than
generic sender rewards. The product should make it easy for the receiver to say "this
worked, use this again."

### Diaspora banking and African fintech partnerships

Nigerian banks actively serve diaspora needs through diaspora banking propositions. This
shows that remittance sits next to broader financial jobs: accounts, investment, property,
schooling, and family support. Partnerships may be useful later, but for MVP they mostly
signal that SocialRemit competes in a crowded trust ecosystem, not a blank market.

## Launch experiments

1. **Community proof pilot** — one Ghanaian and one Nigerian community partner, each with
   a small cohort of users, live transfer support, and post-transfer feedback.
2. **Receiver-method test** — compare messaging for bank payout, mobile money, and cash
   pickup. Measure which one creates most confidence.
3. **Referral trust test** — sender receives an invite/recommendation from someone in the
   community vs from paid ad. Measure signup and first-transfer intent.
4. **Support confidence test** — show users two propositions: "fast and low fee" vs
   "tracked transfer with human help if stuck." See which one earns trial.
5. **Corridor-specific landing pages** — UK to Ghana and UK to Nigeria pages with real
   payout rails, sample rates, local receiver methods, and support promises.

## Proposal implications

VL should not present GTM as a generic digital acquisition funnel. The proposal should
state that the MVP launch plan needs corridor-specific trust anchors and that early
learning should validate community/referral channels before scaling spend.

Minimum GTM artifacts to request from SocialRemit:

- list of Joseph's reachable Ghanaian and Nigerian community contacts;
- existing diaspora/association relationships;
- intended launch geography in the UK;
- appetite for community ambassadors or physical onboarding support;
- receiver-side payout priorities per corridor;
- tolerance for field learning during the 6-8 week delivery window.

## Confidence map

High confidence: community trust and referrals matter; WhatsApp/informal networks matter
for Nigeria; receiver payout methods influence provider choice.

Medium confidence: exact channel ranking; which UK cities should launch first; whether
TikTok/influencers outperform community partnerships.

Low confidence: conversion economics, because no campaign or interview data exists yet.
