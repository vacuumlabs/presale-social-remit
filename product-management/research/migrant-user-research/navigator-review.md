# Navigator review

## Evidence quality

The strongest evidence is around the character of remittance as transnational family care,
the importance of trust, and the need to avoid treating Ghana and Nigeria as one generic
segment. These are supported by academic studies, World Bank corridor data, and UK-Ghana
market research.

The weakest evidence is around exact channel ranking and persona prevalence. Public
social traces are useful for language and pain points, but they are not representative.
The persona dialectic is deliberately synthetic: useful for product discussion, not
validated as market segmentation.

## Gaps

- No primary interviews with UK-based Ghanaian or Nigerian senders.
- No receiver interviews in Ghana or Nigeria.
- No hard data on SocialRemit's reachable launch communities.
- No comparison between Joseph-sourced and independently recruited users.
- No direct test of whether "built by migrants" resonates in user language.
- No data on whether cash-in is necessary for SocialRemit's specific first users.

## Contradictions and tensions

There is a productive tension between digital-first delivery and community-based trust.
The market has mature digital competitors, but informal and community-based channels still
matter because they solve trust, rate, or social proximity problems.

There is also a tension between sender convenience and receiver realities. A sender may
prefer an app, but receiver payout access can decide repeat usage.

Price is contradictory as a differentiator: users clearly care about rates, yet the
Nigeria corridor already appears low-cost in World Bank data. The proposal should not
claim price advantage without corridor-specific proof.

## Blind spots

The research still under-represents lower-literacy, low-income, older, and cash-reliant
users. Public web traces skew digital. If SocialRemit targets low-income migrant workers,
the next research pass must intentionally recruit outside digitally vocal communities.

The research also under-represents women senders and receiver-side decision-making. Family
care literature points to obligation and household dynamics, but the product implications
need direct interviews.

## Follow-up tasks

1. **Interview 3 Ghana + 3 Nigeria senders before proposal finalisation if possible.**
   Test provider choice, trust, receiver method, switching trigger, and reaction to
   "built by migrants."
2. **Ask Joseph for the first launch community map.** Which cities, churches,
   associations, businesses, influencers, and diaspora contacts can he actually reach?
3. **Run a competitor proof check.** For UK -> Ghana and UK -> Nigeria, compare SocialRemit
   intended MVP against Wise, Remitly, WorldRemit, Sendwave, TapTap Send, Western Union,
   and one informal/crypto workaround from the user's eyes.
