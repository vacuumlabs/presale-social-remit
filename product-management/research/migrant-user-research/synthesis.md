# Synthesis — migrant sender research for SocialRemit

## Executive summary

- SocialRemit's core claim, "built by migrants, for migrants", is promising but
  unvalidated. It becomes credible only if translated into corridor-specific trust,
  receiver confidence, and support when money gets stuck.
- UK -> Ghana and UK -> Nigeria should not be treated as one segment. Ghana appears more
  shaped by mobile-money receiver expectations; Nigeria has stronger visible signals of
  informal/networked alternatives and rate-seeking workarounds.
- Price matters, but it is not enough. World Bank data shows UK -> Nigeria is already a
  low-cost competitive corridor, while UK -> Ghana has more cost variation. SocialRemit
  needs reliability, transparency, and trust proof, not only a fee claim.
- The real user is a family system: sender, receiver, and sometimes wider relatives. The
  sender app must account for receiver payout reality and family-pressure dynamics.
- GTM should not be framed as generic digital acquisition. Early launch needs community
  proof: referrals, diaspora groups, faith/community partners, trusted businesses, and
  receiver success stories.

## Key findings

### 1. Remittance is family care under pressure

Academic work on UK-based Ghanaian and Nigerian senders frames remittances as
transnational family care. Senders are often treated as absent providers. Nigerian migrant
research also surfaces conflict: demands, family expectations, unaccountability, and
rivalries around how money is used.

So what: SocialRemit should not design only for a clean "send money" task. It should
design for urgency, proof, accountability, and emotional load.

### 2. Receiver experience is part of sender trust

For Ghana, mobile money is a major receiver-side context. For Nigeria, bank payout,
informal trust networks, and possible crypto/stablecoin workarounds appear more salient
in public traces. In both cases, sender confidence depends on whether the receiver can
actually access the money.

So what: MVP scope should include sender-visible payout confidence, clear receiver method,
and exception handling. This is not merely integration detail.

### 3. Generic fintech trust is weaker than corridor-local trust

Users do not trust "new regulated fintech" in the abstract. They trust a provider because
someone they know used it, the receiver got money, the rate was fair, and support did not
disappear during failure.

So what: proposal positioning should emphasise corridor learning and community proof
rather than only technology delivery.

### 4. Digital-first does not remove offline/community GTM

Even for an app-first product, launch trust may come from churches, associations,
diaspora businesses, WhatsApp groups, referrals, and receiver-side validation. Public
Nigeria discussions show WhatsApp/networked alternatives as real comparison points.

So what: SocialRemit should define first launch communities, not only app features.

### 5. The "migrant-built" claim needs proof points

The dialectic suggests three proof points:

- **Trust proof:** community/reviews/support make the app safer than an unknown newcomer.
- **Receiver proof:** the Ghana/Nigeria last mile works and is visible to the sender.
- **Care proof:** the product reflects urgency, obligation, and family accountability.

So what: "Built by migrants" should be treated as a hypothesis to validate, not a
finished positioning line.

## Hypotheses tested

1. **"Built by migrants" is not enough** — supported. It needs concrete proof.
2. **Price is necessary but not differentiating** — supported, especially for Nigeria.
3. **Trust is corridor-local** — supported.
4. **The sender is not the only user** — supported.
5. **Ghana and Nigeria differ materially** — supported directionally; needs interviews.
6. **Informal channels solve trust/social proximity** — supported for Nigeria; older
   Ghana evidence also points to formal/informal channel complexity.
7. **App-first may need offline trust anchors** — supported.
8. **The emotional job is bigger than speed** — strongly supported.
9. **Crypto/stablecoin is niche pressure** — plausible but low-confidence.
10. **Proposal should sell risk reduction** — supported.

## Proposal implications

VL should frame the SocialRemit build as a fast MVP plus a learning system, not just app
delivery. The credible proposal angle:

> SocialRemit has a strong founder narrative and clear corridor ambition. The risk is
> building a generic remittance app before validating which migrant-specific trust signals
> drive switching. VL can deliver the MVP while instrumenting the product and launch to
> learn quickly from Ghanaian and Nigerian senders and receivers.

Suggested proposal language themes:

- "Corridor-specific trust, not generic remittance UX."
- "Receiver confidence as part of the sender experience."
- "Community-led launch learning, not only paid acquisition."
- "Transparent cost and transfer-state clarity."
- "Exception handling as a trust feature."

## Product implications

MVP should prioritise:

- transparent fee + FX + receiver amount;
- corridor-specific payout method display;
- plain-language transfer states;
- recipient confirmation;
- clear support escalation for stuck transfers;
- post-transfer feedback loop;
- referral/community-source tracking.

MVP should avoid overcommitting to:

- full cash-in network unless validated;
- crypto/stablecoin support for mainstream users;
- broad persona claims without interviews;
- "low-cost" positioning without live corridor comparison.

## Discovery questions to add

- Which exact Ghana/Nigeria payout methods are must-have for launch?
- What first UK communities can SocialRemit reach without paid ads?
- Who are Joseph's community proof points?
- What failure state most damages trust: delay, refund, account lock, receiver cannot
  collect, or unexplained FX?
- Do users understand and trust open-banking funding?
- Is sender-visible recipient confirmation legally/operationally feasible through Fincode
  and local partners?

## Confidence map

Well-supported: emotional/family nature of remittances; need for trust; receiver-side
importance; price insufficiency; corridor differences.

Single-sourced or directional: exact UK-Ghana channel behaviour from older research;
specific prevalence of informal channels; relative importance of TikTok/creators.

Speculative: persona names/details; crypto mainstream relevance; exact conversion impact
of community-led GTM.

## Recommended next steps

1. Use this synthesis to sharpen the proposal around risk reduction and corridor-specific
   trust.
2. Ask Joseph for a concrete first-launch community map.
3. Recruit 6 fast interviews: 3 UK-Ghana senders, 3 UK-Nigeria senders, deliberately not
   all from Joseph's network.
4. Run a competitor proof check from the user's eyes before claiming differentiation.
