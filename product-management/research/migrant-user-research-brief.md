# Migrant User Research Brief — Ghana & Nigeria corridors

**Status: active.** The first desk-research and digital-ethnography pass has been written
up under [`migrant-user-research/`](./migrant-user-research/). This brief defines research
the team agreed is
needed during PM onboarding (2026-05-29). It exists so the need is not lost; it is not
itself the findings.

## Decision this research informs

What must VL know about UK-based Ghanaian and Nigerian migrant senders to make
SocialRemit's MVP, positioning, and launch plan credible in the proposal?

This research is primarily for the VL presale/product team, with proposal reuse as the
secondary audience. The output should help scope decisions, sharpen discovery questions,
and pressure-test positioning. It is not intended to be a polished market report.

## Why this research exists

SocialRemit's entire positioning rests on one claim: **"built by migrants, for migrants"**
and a product that works "the way life actually works" ([product-vision.md](../product-vision.md)).
Today that claim is a *founder narrative*, not a validated *user insight*. Three gaps make
this risky:

1. **No personas exist.** We have a segment label — "UK-based migrants and low-income
   workers sending to Ghana and Nigeria" — but no texture on who they actually are
   ([domain-research.md](../domain-research.md), [client-overview.md](../../deal-context/client-overview.md)).
2. **The founder may not represent the user.** Joseph Owusu is a highly-educated FCCA
   fellow. His intuition about lower-income, lower-literacy senders is a hypothesis to
   test, not a finding to build on.
3. **GTM depends on it.** The "first 10,000 customers" plan and the 6-months-to-seed
   timeline both depend on knowing where these communities are and what moves them —
   currently undocumented.

This research de-risks the product strategy *and* the proposal: it lets VL frame scope and
differentiation around evidence rather than slogans.

## Research objectives

1. **Define 2–4 primary sender personas** for the Ghana and Nigeria corridors — demographics, income, occupation, migration stage, digital literacy, English fluency, device tier.
2. **Map where these communities gather** — online (WhatsApp/Facebook groups, TikTok, Telegram, community radio, diaspora forums) and offline (churches, associations, money-transfer shops, markets) — to inform GTM channels.
3. **Surface real pain points** in how they send money today, and the emotional context around it (trust, shame, obligation, urgency).
4. **Pressure-test the differentiation** — does "built by migrants" resonate, and what concretely would make these users switch from Wise / Western Union / WorldRemit / crypto?
5. **Validate channel assumptions** — is a fully digital experience enough, or is a physical/cash-in/agent touchpoint expected for trust and funding?

## Recon-research scope

The first research run should be a fast desk-research and digital-ethnography pass, not
primary fieldwork. It should cover:

- Sender behaviour and pain points for UK -> Ghana and UK -> Nigeria remittance users.
- Trust and switching logic across formal providers, informal networks, and crypto or
  stablecoin routes where relevant.
- Channel reality: where Ghanaian and Nigerian migrant communities discover, discuss,
  and recommend remittance services.
- Proposal implications: what this changes for SocialRemit's MVP, positioning, GTM
  assumptions, and follow-up discovery questions.

The first run should not attempt a full competitor teardown, full regulatory analysis,
or complete interview protocol. Those can be follow-up workstreams if the evidence shows
they matter.

## Hypotheses to pressure-test

1. **"Built by migrants" is not enough.** Users will care only if the claim translates
   into concrete trust signals: better receiver experience, transparent rates, fewer
   failed transfers, local proof, or community endorsement.
2. **Price is necessary but not differentiating.** Price and FX must be competitive, but
   the market already has strong low-cost players. Switching likely requires price plus
   trust, reliability, and receiver convenience.
3. **Trust is corridor-local, not generic fintech trust.** A new app needs Ghana/Nigeria
   credibility: known payout rails, community references, visible compliance, and proof
   that recipients receive money smoothly.
4. **The sender is not the only user.** Receiver access and preference may decide the
   sender's provider choice. Mobile money, bank payout, cash pickup, and local familiarity
   may matter as much as sender-side UX.
5. **Ghana and Nigeria should not be treated as one segment.** The corridors may differ
   materially in payout rails, community networks, provider expectations, and informal
   transfer behaviour.
6. **Informal channels persist because they solve trust and social proximity.** They may
   survive where formal providers feel impersonal, expensive, over-compliant, or detached
   from local realities.
7. **An app-first product may still need offline trust anchors.** SocialRemit can be
   digital-first, but early GTM may need churches, shops, associations, community leaders,
   or referral loops.
8. **The emotional job is bigger than "send money fast."** Senders may be managing
   obligation, guilt, status, urgency, family negotiation, and fear of being exploited.
9. **Crypto/stablecoin is likely a niche pressure, not a mainstream MVP requirement.**
   It may matter for rate-seeking or financially sophisticated users, while mainstream
   users may prioritize simplicity, reversibility, and trust.
10. **The proposal should sell risk reduction, not only feature delivery.** VL's value
    is helping SocialRemit avoid building a generic remittance app around an untested
    founder narrative.

## Key research questions

### Who they are
- What is the typical sender profile per corridor (Ghana vs Nigeria may differ)? Age, occupation, income band, years in the UK, family structure back home.
- What is their digital literacy and English fluency? Which apps do they already trust and use daily?
- What device and connectivity constraints exist (older Android, prepaid data, shared phones)?

### Behaviour & pain points
- How do they send money today — which providers, how often, what amounts, which delivery method (bank, mobile money, cash pickup)?
- What goes wrong / frustrates them (fees, hidden FX, delays, failed transfers, KYC friction, receiver access)?
- What is the emotional context — is it routine, stressful, obligation-driven, secretive? When in the month does it happen?

### Differentiation & switching
- What do they actually optimise for — cheapest, fastest, most trusted, easiest?
- Does "built by migrants, for migrants" resonate, or read as marketing?
- What would make them switch from their current provider? What would stop them?
- How do they perceive crypto/stablecoin transfers on these corridors (popular, distrusted, irrelevant)?

### Channel & trust
- Would they trust a brand-new app with no high-street presence? What builds that trust?
- Is card / open-banking funding sufficient, or is cash-in a real gap for this segment?
- Who else influences the choice (the receiver, community elders, word of mouth)?

### Go-to-market
- Where do they discover financial apps — referral, community, ads, app store, influencers?
- Which communities, groups, or partnerships could seed a corridor launch?

## Suggested method

This is **lightweight, fast, qualitative-first** research appropriate to a presale — not a
full market study. Recommended:

- **5–10 user interviews** per corridor with real senders (30–45 min, remote or in-person). Recruit via diaspora community groups, money-transfer shops, or Joseph's network — with the caveat that founder-sourced recruits may be biased toward existing fans.
- **Desk research** on UK→Ghana and UK→Nigeria remittance corridors: volumes, average transfer size, dominant providers, mobile-money penetration (e.g. MTN MoMo in Ghana, OPay/PalmPay in Nigeria), and known fee/FX benchmarks. The `recon-research` or `deep-research` skill can accelerate this.
- **Digital ethnography** — observe 3–5 relevant WhatsApp/Facebook/TikTok communities to see how money transfer is discussed, what complaints recur, and who the influencers are.
- **Competitor teardown from the user's eyes** — how Wise, WorldRemit, Remitly, TapTap Send, and a crypto option actually feel for these specific corridors.

## Dependencies & caveats

- **Recruitment bias:** if all interviewees come from Joseph's network, findings will skew positive. Mix in independently sourced participants.
- **Time vs the 6–8 week clock:** full research may not fit the aggressive timeline. Even 3–5 interviews + desk research is far better than zero; scope to what the timeline allows.
